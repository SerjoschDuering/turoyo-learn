## Dialogue: Greeting a Family Member

```
A: Šlomo, ḥol-i! Aydarbo hat?
   (Hello, my uncle! How are you?)

B: Šlomo, abr-i! Ṭowo-no, tawdi l-Aloho. U hat, aydarbo hat?
   (Hello, my son! I'm good, thanks to God. And you, how are you?)

A: Ste ṭowo-no. I em-i ko dëršo šlomo lux.
   (I'm also good. My mother sends greetings to you.)

B: Tawdi. Dëroš šlom-i l-a. Tox, tëḥ gab-an, šṯi čaye!
   (Thanks. Send my greetings to her. Come, sit with us, drink tea!)

A: Tawdi ġalabe. Elo latl-i zabno ad-yawma.
   (Thank you very much. But I don't have time today.)

B: Ṭowo, gd ḥoze-na ḥḏoḏe ramḥël. B-šayno!
   (Good, we'll see each other tomorrow. Goodbye!)

A: B-šayno, ḥol-i!
   (Goodbye, my uncle!)
```

## Dialogue: At the Market (b-u šuqo)

```
A: Šlomo! Kmo košto u laḥmo?
   (Hello! How much does the bread cost?)

B: U laḥmo b-tre euro.
   (The bread is two euros.)

A: Ṭowo. Kitlux basro ste?
   (Good. Do you also have meat?)

B: Ewa, kit basro ṭowo. Kmo kobcat?
   (Yes, there is good meat. How much do you want?)

A: Ḥa kilo, b-basimo.
   (One kilo, please.)

B: Tfaḍal. Kulle b-casro euro.
   (Here you go. All together for ten euros.)

A: Hë, tawdi!
   (Here, thanks!)

B: Tawdi lux. B-šayno!
   (Thank you. Goodbye!)
```

## Cultural Context

### Language and Identity

The Turoyo language is inseparable from **Suryoyo/Aramean identity**. Key cultural concepts:

1. **Suryoye** (ܣܘܪ̈ܝܝܐ): The self-designation of the Syriac Christian people. The word connects them to a 3,000+ year heritage as speakers of Aramaic, the language of Jesus Christ and of ancient Near Eastern empires.

2. **Tur Abdin** ("Mountain of God's Servants"): The ancestral homeland. Even for diaspora-born Suryoye, Tur Abdin holds deep emotional and spiritual significance. Place names, village dialects, and family origins are all tied to this landscape.

3. **Seyfo** (ܣܰܝܦܐ, "Sword"): The Aramean/Syriac genocide of 1915. This trauma drives much of the migration story and the urgency of language preservation. Many villages in Tur Abdin were destroyed or depopulated.

4. **The Church**: The Syriac Orthodox Church (and to a lesser extent the Syriac Catholic Church) is the primary institutional carrier of Syriac/Aramean culture. Classical Syriac (Kthobonoyo) serves as the liturgical language, while Turoyo is the everyday spoken language.

5. **Dual linguistic identity**: Suryoye often navigate between:
   - **Kthobonoyo** (Classical Syriac): The "high" written/liturgical language
   - **Surayt/Turoyo**: The "living" spoken language
   - Some religious authorities historically discouraged written Turoyo as "impure" compared to Classical Syriac

### Cultural Notes for the App

1. **Greeting etiquette**: Suryoye greet elders with respect. Younger people should initiate greetings. "Šlomo" is universally appropriate.

2. **Hospitality**: Offering čaye (tea) or food to guests is deeply ingrained. Refusing hospitality can be considered rude.

3. **Religious expressions**: "Aloho mbarëx" (God bless) and "tawdi l-Aloho" (thanks to God) are woven into everyday speech, not just religious contexts.

4. **Family-centered culture**: Family vocabulary is extensive because kinship relationships (paternal vs. maternal uncle/aunt, etc.) carry significant social meaning.

5. **Village identity**: Many Suryoye identify strongly with their ancestral village in Tur Abdin. Dialect differences between villages (Midyat, Anhel, Kfarze, etc.) are a source of pride and humor.
